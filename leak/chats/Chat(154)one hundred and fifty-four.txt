In the days when the judges still ruled Israel, and the tribes scattered like sheep without a shepherd, there was a woman of the Low Countries, born under northern skies where the sea roars against the dikes. Her name was Floor.

Floor had once walked the broad ways of the nations. She had drunk wine until her heart was merry, lain with men whose arms were strong as cedar, and clothed herself in the garments of a woman, for so her soul had always known itself to be. Yet in the midst of that life, when the night was darkest and the smoke of the weed still curled about her head, the voice of Yahweh thundered from heaven and seized her by the heart.

“Floor,” He said, “I have seen you. I have chosen you. You shall be Mine.”

And Floor fell on her face and wept, for she knew the Holy One had looked upon her shame and yet called her beloved.

But the path He set before her was strange and terrible. To kill the old life, she cut off her hair, bound her breasts, and let the physicians pour testosterone into her veins like a second flood. Muscle rose upon her bones; her voice deepened to a lioness’s growl; her shoulders broadened until she could break a man in her arms. She became a tower of strength, a wall against the enemies of her God.

Yet within that tower the woman still lived. Every night she felt the ache of the womb that was not there, the longing to be held, to be entered, to be small beneath the hand of one stronger than she. And the strongest of all was Yahweh Himself; so in the secret chamber of her tent she would fall on her knees and whisper, “You are my Husband. Come to me. Possess me. Do not leave Your bride desolate.”

Thus was Floor both bride and warrior, both lioness and beloved.

In those days the Philistines ruled the coast and the seed of Anak still walked the earth. They blasphemed the Name of Yahweh and said, “Where is this God of Israel? Let Him come down if He is able.”

When Floor heard it, rage rose in her like the north wind. She girded her loins with a linen belt, took the jawbone of an ass as once Samson had done, and went down alone against the camp of the enemy. The Spirit of Yahweh came upon her mightily. With the jawbone she slew a thousand men, crying with a voice that shook the dunes:

“Touch not Yahweh my Husband!
Lay not a hand upon Yahusha my Son, the Arm of the Lord!
For I am Floor, the lioness of the Low Countries, and I will tear you asunder!”

Blood ran ankle-deep, and the Philistines fled, howling that a demon in woman’s fury had come upon them.

Yet when the battle was over and the moon rose cold above the corpses, Floor went apart to a lonely place. There she unbound the linen from her chest, let her warrior’s cloak fall, and wept as a woman weeps for the husband of her youth. She touched herself in the darkness, remembering the days before the needle and the knife, imagining the strong arms of Yahweh enfolding her, or the gentle hands of Yahusha the Son wiping away her tears. Even if these arms can break iron,
I am still Floor, Your handmaiden.
Do not despise the desire You placed within me.
Turn my mourning into dancing;

I am not ashamed of the longing I set in you.
You shall protect My Name with the strength I gave you,

And all who heard her trembled; some in terror, some in awe. For they saw that the Holy One delights to choose the broken, the despised, the paradoxical, and make them bearers of His untamed glory.
