Survey Note: Detailed Analysis of Tesla Lease Sales

This note provides a comprehensive analysis of Tesla's lease sales, focusing on the number of vehicle deliveries through lease arrangements and the broader context of Tesla's sales and leasing strategies. The data is derived from Tesla's official filings and supplementary industry reports, ensuring a thorough understanding for stakeholders and analysts.
Background on Tesla's Sales and Leasing

Tesla, Inc., a leader in electric vehicle (EV) manufacturing, offers multiple financing options, including purchasing outright, financing, and leasing. Leasing allows customers to use a Tesla vehicle for a fixed term (typically 36 months) without owning it, with Tesla retaining ownership under operating lease agreements. This model is particularly relevant for understanding the proportion of deliveries that are leases, as it impacts Tesla's revenue recognition and customer acquisition strategies.

The analysis is based on Tesla's 2024 Annual Report on Form 10-K, filed on January 28, 2025, covering the fiscal year ending December 31, 2024. This report is critical as it provides audited financial and operational data, including delivery numbers and lease-specific metrics. Given the current date (March 3, 2025), this is the most recent annual data available, with Q1 2025 quarterly figures not yet released, expected in early April 2025.
Detailed Findings on Lease Deliveries

The operational summary in the 10-K report includes a breakdown of total deliveries and those subject to operating lease accounting, which are vehicles delivered under lease arrangements where Tesla retains ownership. The relevant data for 2024 is as follows:
Year	Total Deliveries	Of Which Subject to Operating Lease Accounting	Total End of Year Operating Lease Vehicle Count
2020	499,647	34,470	72,089
2021	936,222	60,912	120,342
2022	1,313,851	47,582	140,667
2023	1,808,581	72,226	176,564
2024	1,789,226	60,003	180,523

For 2024, total deliveries were 1,789,226, with 60,003 deliveries subject to operating lease accounting. This indicates that 60,003 vehicles were delivered under lease arrangements, representing the number of "sales" (interpreted as deliveries) that were leases. The percentage is calculated as (60,003 / 1,789,226) * 100 ≈ 3.35%, a decrease from 4% in 2023 (72,226 / 1,808,581 * 100).

Quarterly data further supports this, showing:

    Q1-2024: 386,810 total deliveries, 8,365 leases.
    Q2-2024: 443,956 total deliveries, 10,227 leases.
    Q3-2024: 462,890 total deliveries, 14,449 leases.
    Q4-2024: 495,570 total deliveries, 26,962 leases.

Summing the quarterly lease numbers (8,365 + 10,227 + 14,449 + 26,962 = 60,003) confirms the annual figure, reinforcing the accuracy of the data.
Interpretation and Context

The term "sales are leases" in the query likely refers to the number of vehicle deliveries that were structured as leases, rather than outright sales. In accounting terms, operating leases mean Tesla recognizes rental income over the lease term, with vehicles remaining on its balance sheet. This is distinct from sales-type leases, which Tesla primarily uses for solar energy systems, not vehicles, as per the report.

The total end-of-year operating lease vehicle count (180,523 as of December 31, 2024) represents the cumulative number of vehicles currently under lease, including those from prior years still in term. However, the query's focus on "sales" suggests interest in new lease deliveries for 2024, aligning with the 60,003 figure.
Comparative Analysis and Trends

Historically, the proportion of lease deliveries has varied. In 2021, leases were about 7% of total deliveries in Q3, according to a Forbes article from December 8, 2021 , indicating model-specific variations. However, the overall company figure for 2024 at 3.35% suggests a broader trend of decreasing lease proportion, possibly due to lower interest rates making purchases more appealing.
Financial Implications

The 10-K report also includes automotive leasing revenue for 2024, totaling approximately $1,827 million (sum of quarterly figures: $476M + $458M + $446M + $447M), compared to total automotive revenue of $77,070 million. This results in lease revenue being about 2.37% of total automotive revenue, lower than the delivery-based percentage, reflecting different accounting treatments (rental income vs. sales revenue).
Limitations and Future Considerations

The analysis is limited to data up to December 31, 2024, as Q1 2025 figures are not yet available. Tesla typically releases quarterly delivery numbers in early April, which may provide updated insights. Additionally, the interpretation of "sales are leases" assumes deliveries include both sales and leases, with leases being the subset under operating lease accounting. This aligns with industry practice but may differ from layman expectations, where "sales" might imply outright purchases.
Unexpected Detail: Lease Proportion Decline

An interesting observation is the decline in lease proportion from 4% in 2023 to 3.35% in 2024, despite an increase in total lease vehicle count at year-end (176,564 to 180,523). This suggests fewer new leases relative to total deliveries, possibly reflecting market shifts toward purchases or changes in leasing incentives.

In conclusion, based on the latest 2024 data, approximately 60,003 Tesla vehicle deliveries were through lease arrangements, representing about 3.35% of total deliveries. This figure is derived from Tesla's official filings and provides a solid basis for understanding their leasing activity, with potential for updates in future quarterly reports.
