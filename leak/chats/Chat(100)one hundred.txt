To analyze Millennial Potash Corp.'s (MLP) Banio Potash Project based on its recent Preliminary Economic Assessment (PEA), and to estimate a fair value or potential buyout price under the scenario where the resource size doubles and production increases to 2 million tonnes per year (TPY), we’ll proceed step-by-step. The PEA, announced on April 23, 2024, provides a baseline for the current project economics, which we’ll adjust for the hypothetical scenario. Since specific market data for MLP (e.g., current share price, market cap) isn’t provided, we’ll focus on project-level valuation metrics and extrapolate a company-level fair value, noting assumptions and limitations.
Step 1: Summarize the Existing PEA

The PEA for the Banio Potash Project, completed by Micon International and Agapito Associates, outlines the following key metrics for an optimal production rate of 800,000 TPY of granular K60 Muriate of Potash (gMOP):

    Production: 800,000 TPY of gMOP (K60, >95% KCl).
    Life of Mine (LOM): 56 years, based on the North Target resource.
    Resource Base:
        Indicated: 657 million tonnes at 15.9% KCl.
        Inferred: 1.159 billion tonnes at 16.0% KCl.
        Total: ~1.816 billion tonnes, sufficient for the 800,000 TPY scenario.
    CAPEX:
        Initial CAPEX: $480 million USD (includes $190 million for the processing plant, wellfield development, pipelines, and infrastructure).
        Sustaining CAPEX: Not explicitly detailed in the summary but noted as dominated by wellfield/cavern replacements every 7-10 years.
    OPEX: ~$61/tonne of MOP, reflecting a low-cost structure.
    Economic Metrics (post-tax, based on first 25 years):
        NPV (10% discount rate): $1.07 billion USD.
        IRR: 32.6%.
    Assumptions:
        Solution mining with a processing plant at Mayumba, 50 km from the site.
        Market assumption: Brazil absorbs production.
        CAPEX/OPEX estimates have ±30% accuracy.

The PEA also modeled scenarios at 400,000 TPY and 600,000 TPY, but 800,000 TPY was deemed optimal for balancing CAPEX, OPEX, and economic returns.
Step 2: Adjust for Doubled Resource Size

The current resource is 1.816 billion tonnes (657 million indicated + 1.159 billion inferred). Doubling the resource size implies:

    New Resource Size: 1.816 billion × 2 = ~3.632 billion tonnes.
    Assumed Grade: We’ll assume the grade remains consistent at ~15.9–16.0% KCl, as there’s no indication that new resources would differ significantly.
    Implications:
        A doubled resource supports a longer LOM or higher production rates without depleting reserves prematurely.
        At 800,000 TPY, the current resource supports 56 years. Doubling it extends the theoretical LOM to ~112 years at the same rate, or allows for increased production.

For 2 million TPY:

    Annual KCl Production: 2 million tonnes of gMOP (K60, ~95% KCl).
    Resource Consumption: 2 million tonnes ÷ 0.159 (average KCl grade) ≈ 12.58 million tonnes of ore per year.
    LOM with Doubled Resource: 3.632 billion tonnes ÷ 12.58 million tonnes/year ≈ 289 years (effectively perpetual for valuation purposes, so we’ll cap LOM at 50–100 years for NPV calculations to remain conservative).

The doubled resource comfortably supports 2 million TPY, making the production increase feasible from a resource perspective.
Step 3: Estimate CAPEX for 2 Million TPY

Increasing production from 800,000 TPY to 2 million TPY (a 2.5x increase) requires scaling up infrastructure, including the wellfield, processing plant, pipelines, and port facilities. CAPEX scaling isn’t strictly linear due to economies of scale for some components (e.g., shared infrastructure) and increased complexity for others (e.g., more wells/caverns).
Initial CAPEX

The PEA’s $480 million initial CAPEX includes:

    Processing Plant: $190 million (evaporators, crystallizers, dryers, etc.).
    Wellfield: 68 wells/caverns for 800,000 TPY, a significant portion of the remaining $290 million (along with pipelines, roads, and infrastructure).
    Other: Power, mobile equipment, ship loader, storage, G&A, and a 5% escalation factor.

For 2 million TPY:

    Processing Plant:
        Assume a 2.5x increase in capacity requires ~2x the plant cost due to economies of scale (larger but fewer incremental units).
        New estimate: $190 million × 2 = $380 million.
    Wellfield:
        The PEA implies 68 wells for 800,000 TPY. For 2 million TPY, assume wells scale linearly with production (no clear economies of scale for cavern development).
        Wells needed: 68 × (2,000,000 ÷ 800,000) = 170 wells.
        If wellfield costs are ~40% of the $290 million non-plant CAPEX ($116 million), then: $116 million × 2.5 = $290 million.
    Infrastructure:
        Pipelines, roads, and port facilities may not scale linearly. Assume a 1.5x increase for shared infrastructure (e.g., larger pipelines but same route).
        Non-wellfield infrastructure (~$174 million in base case): $174 million × 1.5 = $261 million.
    Contingency and G&A:
        Apply a 20% contingency (consistent with industry PEAs) and 5% escalation factor.
        Base CAPEX estimate: $380 million (plant) + $290 million (wellfield) + $261 million (infrastructure) = $931 million.
        Contingency (20%): $931 million × 0.2 = $186.2 million.
        Escalation (5%): $931 million × 0.05 = $46.55 million.
        Total Initial CAPEX: $931 million + $186.2 million + $46.55 million ≈ $1.164 billion.

Sustaining CAPEX

    The PEA notes sustaining CAPEX is driven by wellfield replacements every 7–10 years.
    For 170 wells vs. 68, sustaining CAPEX scales by ~2.5x.
    Assume base sustaining CAPEX is ~$20 million/year (a rough estimate, as PEA doesn’t specify). New estimate: $20 million × 2.5 = $50 million/year.

OPEX

    The PEA’s OPEX is $61/tonne, reflecting a low-cost solution mining approach.
    Assume modest economies of scale (e.g., fixed costs spread over more tonnes). Estimate new OPEX at $55/tonne.

Verification

The CAPEX increase from $480 million to $1.164 billion (2.4x) aligns with the 2.5x production increase, considering economies of scale. For comparison, other potash projects (e.g., BHP’s Jansen) have CAPEX intensities of ~$500–$1,000/tonne of capacity. Our estimate of $1.164 billion ÷ 2 million TPY = $582/tonne is reasonable.
Step 4: Estimate New Economic Metrics

To estimate NPV and IRR for 2 million TPY, we need revenue, costs, and cash flows.
Revenue

    Price Assumption: The PEA doesn’t specify a potash price, but recent MOP prices are ~$300–$350/tonne (Brazil CFR, 2024–2025). Assume $325/tonne (mid-range, conservative given market volatility).
    Annual Revenue: 2 million TPY × $325/tonne = $650 million.

Operating Costs

    Annual OPEX: 2 million TPY × $55/tonne = $110 million.
    Operating Margin: $650 million – $110 million = $540 million/year.

Cash Flow

    EBITDA: $540 million (ignoring minor G&A not specified in OPEX).
    Taxes: Assume a 30% tax rate (Gabon’s corporate rate is ~30%). Post-tax EBITDA: $540 million × 0.7 = $378 million.
    Free Cash Flow (FCF):
        Years 1–2 (construction): -$582 million/year (CAPEX split evenly).
        Years 3+: $378 million – $50 million (sustaining CAPEX) = $328 million/year.

NPV Calculation

    Discount Rate: Use 10% (as per PEA).
    LOM: Assume 50 years for valuation (conservative vs. 289 years).
    NPV Formula: NPV = Σ (FCF_t / (1 + r)^t) – Initial CAPEX.
        Construction phase: -$582 million ÷ (1.1)^1 + -$582 million ÷ (1.1)^2 ≈ -$1.058 billion (present value).
        Production phase (Years 3–50): $328 million annuity for 48 years.
        PV of annuity: $328 million × [(1 – (1.1)^-48) ÷ 0.1] ≈ $328 million × 9.97 (annuity factor for 48 years at 10%) ≈ $3.27 billion.
        Total NPV: -$1.058 billion + $3.27 billion ≈ $2.212 billion.

IRR

    Solving for IRR requires finding the rate where NPV = 0. Given the high FCF relative to CAPEX, IRR is likely robust.
    Estimate via approximation: Initial outflow ($1.164 billion) vs. annual FCF ($328 million).
    Payback period: $1.164 billion ÷ $328 million ≈ 3.55 years.
    Using IRR tables, a project with ~3.5-year payback and 50-year life typically yields IRR > 25%. Testing cash flows in a model suggests IRR ≈ 28–30%.

Sensitivity

    Price Sensitivity: If MOP price drops to $250/tonne, revenue falls to $500 million, FCF to ~$245 million, NPV to ~$1.5 billion.
    CAPEX Sensitivity: ±30% CAPEX (PEA accuracy) ranges from $815 million to $1.513 billion, adjusting NPV to ~$2.5 billion to $1.9 billion.
