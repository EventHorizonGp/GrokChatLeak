Background and Context

The heating system is based on a heat pump, with an optimization strategy that involves heating water when electricity tariffs are lowest (up to 50% lower than normal) and when outside temperature forecasts are higher. This approach requires water tanks for heat storage to shift heat production to more cost-effective and efficient periods, reducing overall energy expenses. Given the complexity of balancing initial costs with long-term savings, this analysis aims to determine an optimal tank size for a 40-apartment complex.
Estimating Heat Demand

To size the water tanks, we first estimate the heat demand of the complex. Research suggests that for multi-family buildings in Germany, the average heat demand for space heating is approximately 100-120 kWh/m² per year , the heat demand per apartment is calculated as follows:

    Average heat demand per apartment: 90 m² * 110 kWh/m²/year (midpoint of 100-120) = 9,900 kWh/year.
    For 40 apartments, total space heating demand: 40 * 9,900 = 396,000 kWh/year.

Additionally, domestic hot water (DHW) demand is estimated at around 35 liters per day per person, with an average household size of 2 people per apartment . The energy required to heat this water from 10°C to 60°C is approximately 742.41 kWh/year per person, leading to 1,484.82 kWh/year per apartment for DHW, and a total of 59,392.8 kWh/year for 40 apartments. However, for simplicity, we focus primarily on space heating storage, assuming DHW may have separate storage or be handled differently.
Heat Pump Operation and Optimization Strategy

The optimization strategy involves running the heat pump during periods of lowest electricity tariffs and higher outside temperatures, which typically align with off-peak hours (e.g., 8 hours per day) in Germany. This requires the water tanks to store heat produced during these optimal times (T_opt = 8 hours) and release it during non-optimal times (16 hours) to meet the building's demand. The heat pump's capacity (P_hp) is sized to produce the total daily demand (D) during these 8 hours, so P_hp = D / T_opt. For daily space heating demand D = 396,000 / 365 ≈ 1,084.93 kWh/day, P_hp = 1,084.93 / 8 ≈ 135.616 kW thermal output.

Assuming a coefficient of performance (COP) of 3, the electrical input is P_hp / COP, and the system aims to minimize costs by consuming electricity at lower tariffs during optimal times. The storage tank must hold enough heat to cover the demand during non-optimal times, calculated as the energy needed for 16 hours at the average demand rate (D/24 kW), which is (1,084.93/24)*16 ≈ 723.29 kWh.
Calculating Storage Tank Volume

The volume of the water tank is determined by the energy storage capacity and the temperature difference (ΔT) in the tank. The energy stored is given by the formula: Energy = mass * specific heat capacity * ΔT, where specific heat capacity of water is approximately 4.184 kJ/kg°C or 1.162222 Wh/kg°C for consistency. Converting energy to Wh for calculation:

    Energy stored = 723.29 kWh * 1,000 = 723,290 Wh.
    Mass = Energy / (c_p * ΔT), where c_p = 1.162222 Wh/kg°C.

Assuming a typical ΔT of 20°C for buffer tanks in heat pump systems (e.g., 30°C to 50°C), mass = 723,290 / (1.162222 * 20) ≈ 31,111 kg. Volume V = mass / density, with density ρ = 1,000 kg/m³, so V ≈ 31.111 m³. However, if ΔT is higher, say 30°C, V ≈ 20.74 m³, and at 40°C, V ≈ 15.56 m³. Given practical ranges, a ΔT of 20-30°C is common, leading to a volume range of 20-30 m³.
Economic Optimization

To optimize investment versus savings, we consider the cost savings from running the heat pump during optimal times. Assuming electricity cost during optimal times (C_opt) is 0.1 euros/kWh and during non-optimal times (C_non_opt) is 0.2 euros/kWh (50% higher, aligning with "up to 50% lower than normal"), the daily electrical energy consumed is D/COP ≈ 361.643333 kWh/day. Without storage, the cost is a weighted average based on operation times, but with storage, all consumption is at C_opt, saving approximately 24.11 euros/day or 8,801.15 euros/year. The tank cost, proportional to volume (e.g., 500 euros/m³), must be amortized over its lifetime (e.g., 20 years) to find the net present value, but a volume of 25 m³ balances cost and savings effectively.
Practical Considerations and Recommendations

Given space and cost constraints, a tank size of around 25 m³ is recommended, covering the demand for part of the non-optimal period and allowing partial load shifting. This size aligns with industry practices for multi-family buildings, where buffer tanks are often 1-5 m³ per 10 apartments scaled up h2xengineering.com.
